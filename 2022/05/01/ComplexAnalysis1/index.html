Complex analysis

Some interesting things to learn

Integrals. Many kinds of functions do not have perfect antiderivative such as
$$
\int_{}^{}\frac{1}{1+x^4} dx
$$
but might have nice integrals over good regions
$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{1}{1+x^4} dx
$$

So instead of going from an arbitrary antiderivative from the function we might do something easily for the integer on a complex plane. Residue theorem convert the integrals into algebra.

Fourier transform
$$
\mathcal{F}(f(t))=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)e^{-2\pi ixt}dx
$$
we have already learned Fourier series in the mathematical analysis, Fourier transform is an extension of the Fourier series and a tool of integers and probability.

Factorial Gamma function
$$
(s-1)!=\Gamma(s)=\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-x}x^{s-1}dx , \\
\Gamma (s+1)=s\Gamma(s),\Gamma(\frac{1}{2})=\sqrt\pi
$$
Number theorem: Riemann zeta function and Euler product
$$
\zeta (s)= \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^s}=\prod_{p \in P}(1-\frac{1}{p^s})^{-1},\Re(s)>1
$$
in the mathematical analysis we have already proved
$$
\zeta(2) \quad and \quad \zeta(2n)
$$
in complex analysis ,it has more applications.

Cauchy theorem
$$
\oint f(z)dz=0
$$
when f is the analytic function.

1. Complex differentiable

$$
\lim_{h \rightarrow 0}\frac{f(z+h)-f(z)}{h}=f’(z)
$$

Example:
$$
f(z)=\bar z=\overline{x+iy}=x-iy \\
\lim_{h \rightarrow 0}\frac{f(z+h)-f(z)}{h}=\lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{\bar h}{h} \\
<1>:h \in \mathbb{R}: \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{\bar h}{h}=1 \\
<2> h \in i\mathbb{R}(h=ik,k \in \mathbb{R}) \lim_{k \rightarrow 0} \frac{ -ik}{ik}=-1
$$
In order to make the function

$$
f(z)= \bar z :\mathbb C \rightarrow \mathbb C
$$
differentiable, it has to make sure the limit exist in all of the path. Obviously, it is not equal when h in the real number and the imagine number which is not differentiable.

Now here is a theorem that shows the equivalent way to determine a complex function is differentiable or not.

The complex variable z can be written as
$$
z=x+iy
$$
so that could change function f in to
$$
f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)
$$
assume f complex differentiable find condition for u and v.

In real axis: $h=h \in \mathbb{R}$

$$
f’(z)=\lim_{h \rightarrow 0}\frac{u(x+h,y)+iv(x+h,y)-u(x,y)-iv(x,y)}{h} = \\
\lim_{h \rightarrow 0}\frac{u(x+h,y)-u(x,y)}{h}+i\lim_{h \rightarrow 0}\frac{v(x+h,y)-v(x,y)}{h} \\
=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+i\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}=u_x+iv_x
$$

In imagine axis:

$$
h \in i \mathbb{R},h=ik
$$

$$
f’(z)=\lim_{k \rightarrow 0}\frac{u(x,y+k)+iv(x,y+k)-u(x,y)-iv(x,y)}{ik} \\
\lim_{k \rightarrow 0}\frac{u(x,y+k)-u(x,y)}{ik}+\lim_{k \rightarrow 0}\frac{v(x,y+k)-v(x,y)}{k} \\
$$

$$
=-i\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=v_y-iu_y
$$

get the Cauchy Riemann equation:
$$
f’(z)=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+i\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}=-i\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\\
\boxed{\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial v}{\partial y},\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}=-\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}}
$$
